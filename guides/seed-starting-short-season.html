Seed Starting Schedule for Short Growing Seasons

A simple, reliable method for planning indoor starts and transplant timing.

In a short growing season, timing matters more than almost anything else. Starting too late limits harvest. Starting too early creates stressed, overgrown seedlings.

The goal isn’t to guess. It’s to plan backward from your average last frost date and build a clear window for:

  • Starting seeds indoors
  • Hardening off
  • Transplanting or direct sowing

The 3 Dates That Matter

1. Average Last Frost Date

This anchors your spring schedule. Most warm-season crops are transplanted 1–3 weeks after this date.

2. Indoor Start Window

Many warm-season crops need a 6–10 week head start indoors. Count backward from your last frost date to determine when to sow.

3. Hardening Off Period

Plan 7–10 days before transplanting to gradually expose seedlings to outdoor conditions. Skipping this step can slow growth significantly.

Which Crops Should You Start Indoors?

Usually started indoors:

  • Tomatoes
  • Peppers
  • Broccoli
  • Cabbage
  • Kale

Usually direct sown:

  • Spinach
  • Beans
  • Carrots
  • Radishes

In shorter seasons, starting warm-season crops indoors is often the difference between harvest and disappointment.

Common Short-Season Mistakes

  • Starting too late. Waiting until spring feels safe, but it shortens your harvest window.
  • Starting too early. Overgrown seedlings struggle after transplant.
  • Skipping hardening off. Sudden outdoor exposure causes shock.
  • Growing too many long-season crops. Prioritize what reliably finishes in your climate.

A Practical Planning Method

  1. Identify your average last frost date.
  2. List the crops you want to grow.
  3. Mark indoor start weeks (6–10 weeks before frost for warm-season crops).
  4. Add 7–10 days for hardening off.
  5. Set transplant or direct sow windows.
